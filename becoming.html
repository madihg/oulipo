The World

Becoming Everyone is a speculative fiction performance set in 2046 New Beirut. A yellow mold — physarum polycephalum — has colonised the junction between brain-computer interface devices and the brains of their users. The result is involuntary, rapid personality switching: one person cycling through distinct selves every ten minutes.

One actor performs live on stage, glitching between personas: a breathless news reporter, an army general, a French philosopher, a gallerist, a tech visionary pitching griefbots, a senator, a friend who recognizes the condition as migration. Between them, Omar — patient zero — surfaces, searching.

The climax reveals the mold's origin. The contagion vector was never biological. It was maternal. Omar becomes his mother, who speaks in Lebanese Arabic. She explains: she loved him so fiercely she changed his composition. Made him impossible to decipher. Made him everyone.

The performance blends live action with prerecorded cinematic projection, screen-based text, and dialogue in English, French, Lebanese Arabic, and Spanish. The piece is modular — built from self-contained scenes that can be reordered, expanded, or compressed. In festival contexts, the audience directs the sequence: voting on which persona appears next. No two performances are identical.

Form

Medium

Solo performance with prerecorded video projection, live lighting, multilingual dialogue

Languages

English, French, Lebanese Arabic, Spanish — subtitled on screen

Duration

Modular: 20–55 minutes depending on context. Full work ~50 min.

Structure

15 self-contained scenes. Audience-directed sequencing available for festival presentations.

Space

Intimate black box or gallery. 40–80 seats. Proximity matters — the audience sees the face shift.

Stage

One actor. One screen. One chair. Open floor. The body does the rest.
